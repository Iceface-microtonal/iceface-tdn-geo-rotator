1. Overview

TDN Geo-Rotating Arpeggiator is a kinetic music instrument. Notes are not triggered by pressing keys — they are triggered by movement. The distance your cursor (or finger) travels across the canvas determines when the next note fires.

The canvas displays a cloth-like mesh that responds to drag interaction with realistic physics — stretching, wrinkling, and springing back. This physical deformation is not just visual: it directly modulates pitch, velocity, filter, and timing in real time.

The instrument is built around a single theoretical principle: T(D,N), a dual transformation that simultaneously alters both the timing and pitch of non-diatonic notes based on a single parameter, N.

At N = 1.0, the instrument behaves like standard equal temperament — all notes are equal in both pitch and timing. As N decreases toward 0, non-diatonic notes (black keys) become both shorter in duration and closer in pitch to their diatonic neighbors.

2. How to Play

Auto Play

Click the ▶ PLAY button in the sidebar. The cursor follows a geometric path automatically, generating notes as it moves. Select the path shape from the dropdown next to the Play button. Adjust Speed to control how fast the cursor moves.

Manual Play (Field Drag)

Click and drag anywhere on the canvas. The cloth mesh deforms under your finger/cursor. Notes trigger as the auto-play cursor moves, while your drag modulates the sound through the Field Drag system — warping pitch, velocity, filter, and swing in real time. Releasing the mouse lets the cloth spring back.

4. T(D,N) — The Core Principle

T(D,N) stands for Temporal-Diatonic transformation with parameter N. It is the defining characteristic of this instrument — a single slider that simultaneously controls two musical dimensions.

The Diatonic Reference Set D

D is the set of pitch classes corresponding to the white keys of a standard keyboard:

D = { 0, 2, 4, 5, 7, 9, 11 } → C, D, E, F, G, A, B

This is a topological reference, not a tonal scale. It defines which notes are diatonic (inside D) and which are non-diatonic (outside D — the black keys).

The N Parameter

N ranges from 0 to 1. It controls two things simultaneously for every non-diatonic note:

DimensionDiatonic noteNon-diatonic note
Time (step distance)full stepSizestepSize × N
Pitch (cents offset)0¢+ (1 − N) × 100¢
Example — N = 0.5, Iceface Tuning:
A C# (non-diatonic) will fire after half the normal movement distance, and its pitch will be shifted +50 cents toward D. At the same time, the Iceface tuning preset shifts C# by +50¢ from its equal-tempered position.

Both transformations converge at the same value — the pitch and time compression are unified by a single N.

Z-Depth

Controls how strongly the T(D,N) velocity and filter modulation affect the sound. At Z-Depth = 0, all notes play at equal volume and brightness regardless of their diatonic status. At Z-Depth = 100%, non-diatonic notes at low N values become very quiet and dark — pushed deep into the background, like distant layers in a 3D space.

Swing

Applies rhythmic swing to the step timing. At 50% (center), all steps are even. Increasing swing makes even-numbered steps shorter and odd-numbered steps longer, creating a shuffle or groove feel. The Field Drag system can modulate swing in real time.

T(D,N) Mode vs T(D,N) LEAP Mode

T(D,N) — The original transformation. Step timing is determined by the identity of the current note (is it in D or not?).

T(D,N) LEAP — An extended mode. Step timing is determined by the distance (in semitones) between the current note and the previous note. Larger intervals get longer durations; adjacent notes move quickly.

T(D,N) mode: diatonic note → stepSize × 1.0 non-diatonic → stepSize × N T(D,N) LEAP mode: interval = |current − previous| semitones stretch = 1.0 + (interval − 1) / 10 → 1st: 1.0×, 11th: 2.0× step = stepSize × (1 + (stretch−1) × N)

In LEAP mode, N scales the degree of stretch. At N = 0, all steps are equal regardless of interval. At N = 1.0, large leaps breathe naturally while stepwise motion flows quickly — a principle common to expressive melodic performance.

Base Note

The lowest note of the generated scale (range: C2 to C6). The full note array spans upward from this note for the number of octaves specified.

Octaves

The number of octaves the note array covers (1 to 4). A wider spread gives a larger range and more variety in melodic movement.

6. Synth

Wave

The oscillator waveform. Sine is the purest tone. Triangle is similar but slightly brighter. Square has strong odd harmonics — hollow and reedy. Sawtooth is the richest, with all harmonics present — the classic acid bass basis.

Cutoff

Low-pass filter cutoff frequency. Lower values darken the tone; higher values open up the full harmonic content. The Field Drag system can modulate this in real time.

Resonance

Emphasis at the cutoff frequency. Higher values produce a pronounced peak — the characteristic "wah" of acid bass sounds.

Reverb

Convolution reverb send amount. Adds spatial depth and sustain to the synth voice. At 0%, the sound is completely dry. Higher values create a wide, immersive ambience.

Glide (Portamento)

Smooths pitch transitions between notes. At 0, notes change instantly. As Glide increases, the oscillator slides from the previous pitch to the new one. Creates legato and portamento effects.

Octave

Shifts the entire note array up or down by octaves (−2 to +2). Negative values produce bass register sounds.

Volume

The output level of the synthesizer voice.

7. Drums

The drum engine synthesizes three voices from scratch using the Web Audio API — no samples are used.

Drum steps advance in sync with note triggers — each time the kinetic engine fires a note, the drum sequencer advances one step. The Field Drag system modulates drum velocity and filter in real time.

Volume

Master level for all drum voices.

Filter

Low-pass filter applied to the drum bus independently of the synth. Useful for dialing in the drum tone without affecting the melodic content.

9. Kinetic Parameters

Step

The base distance threshold (in canvas-fraction units) that the cursor must travel before triggering the next note. Smaller values trigger notes more rapidly for the same cursor speed. This value is modified by T(D,N) for non-diatonic notes.

Speed

Only active during Auto Play. Controls how fast the cursor moves along the geometric path. Higher values produce faster, denser playback; lower values create slower, spacious phrasing.

10. Field Drag — Cloth Physics

The canvas displays a woven cloth mesh. When you click and drag on it, the fabric deforms with realistic physics — stretching, wrinkling, and springing back when released. This is the defining interaction model of the Geo-Rotating Arpeggiator.

Field Drag is not just visual. The physical state of the cloth directly modulates the music in real time. Pulling the fabric changes how the instrument sounds — even while Auto Play is running.

How It Works

When you press and drag on the canvas, the system tracks:

  • Anchor point — where you first pressed
  • Cloth displacement — how far the fabric has been pulled (with spring physics and damping)
  • Strain — the tension between your raw drag and the cloth's current position
  • Speed — how fast you are moving
  • Energy — a composite value that rises during active dragging and decays smoothly on release

What It Modulates

ParameterSourceEffect
X WarpHorizontal cloth displacementModulates swing timing offset
ToneVertical cloth displacementModulates synth filter cutoff and drum filter. Drag up = brighter, drag down = darker.
DepthEnergy + strainAdds Z-Depth lift — affects velocity and spatial depth
EnergyComposite of all drag forcesOverall modulation intensity. Drives velocity boost, kinetic acceleration, and visual effects.

The Field Drag display in the sidebar shows the current values of X Warp, Tone, Depth, and Energy in real time.

Cloth Spring Behavior

The cloth uses a spring-damper model. When you pull and release, it doesn't snap back instantly — it oscillates and settles. This means the sound modulation continues briefly after you release, creating organic, decaying expression. The heavier the drag, the slower the spring constant, giving a weighty, fabric-like feel.

12. BPM Display

The header bar shows an estimated tempo in the format ~ 120 TEMPO. This is calculated from the average interval between recent note triggers. Because timing in this instrument is spatial rather than clock-based, the BPM fluctuates with cursor speed, Field Drag energy, and step size. It serves as a reference, not a fixed metronome.

Note on timing: Drum steps and note triggers are tied together — each note trigger advances the drum sequencer by one step. This means the drum pattern tempo is entirely determined by your cursor movement speed and the kinetic step size, not by a clock. This is fundamental to T(D,N): time is spatial, not metronomic.
